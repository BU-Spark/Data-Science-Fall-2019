    03/08/2017 #3 ORDER: Before me is the plaintiff's petition, pursuant to G. L. c. 231, § 118, par. 1, seeking reversal of an order of a Superior Court judge, denying the plaintiff's motion for a temporary restraining order. After review of the petition and the materials filed with it, the petition is hereby denied. Though the single justice possesses the plenary authority to review the trial court's action on an application for preliminary injunctive relief, doing so is a matter of single justice discretion. "In most cases, based on the deference normally accorded determinations by the judge who heard the matter in the first instance, the single justice will decline to act on an application for relief under G. L. c. 231, § 118, first par., that does not disclose clear error of law or abuse of discretion." Jet-Line Services, Inc. v. Board of Selectmen of Stoughton, 25 Mass. App. Ct. 645, 646 (1988). See also Planned Parenthood League of Massachusetts, Inc. v. Operation Rescue, 406 Mass. 701, 709-710 (1990). "[S]ingle justices ... exercise[ ] their discretion [at the preliminary injunction stage] sparingly and only in situations where a petitioner has shown clear entitlement to relief." Edwin R. Sage Co. v. Foley, 12 Mass. App. Ct. 20, 25, (1981). If the trial court "applied proper legal standards and . . . there was reasonable support for its evaluation of factual questions," there is no abuse of discretion. Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney, 380 Mass. 609, 615 (1980); Biotti v. Board of Selectmen of Manchester, 25 Mass. App. Ct. 637, 640 (1988). Indeed, in such a discretionary context, if there is any supportable basis for the judge's action, the appellate court will not second-guess the judge or intervene to reverse the ruling below, even if it might have decided the matter differently in the first instance, and even if the preliminary injunction may ultimately turn out to have been mistaken, Westinghouse Bdcst. Co. v. New England Patriots Football Club, Inc., 10 Mass. App. Ct. 70, 75 (1980); Carabetta Enterprises, Inc. v. Schena, 25 Mass. App. Ct. 389, 392 (1988), unless the single justice (or other appropriate appellate tribunal) is clearly persuaded "that no conscientious judge, acting intelligently, could honestly have taken the view expressed by him." Mazzoleni v. Cotton, 33 Mass. App. Ct. 147, 152-153 (1992) (citations omitted).[1] In the present case, the petitioner plaintiff has failed to sustain its formidable burden. Though the petitioner suggests that the Superior Court judge merely "rubber stamped" a flawed decision by the hearing officer in the Office of the Attorney General, her careful and thorough memorandum of decision reflects independent consideration of the question presented. More to the point, I discern no clear error in her reasoning that the defendant Southeastern Regional Services Group (SERSG) acted within its statutory authority pursuant to G. L. c. 30, § 30M(a), when it determined that it was in the public interest to reject all bids and re-open the bidding process. As the Superior Court judge observed, G. L. c. 30, § 39M(a), allows a contract-awarding authority to reject any and all bids, thereby re-opening the bidding process, if it is in the public interest to do so. Though the contracting authority must be able to articulate a legitimate rationale for rejecting all bids - a bare desire to get a better price is not enough, see Petricca Constr. Co. v. Commonwealth, 37 Mass. App. Ct. 392, 398 (1994) - the authority is vested with a measure of discretion in assessing the public interest. See Annese Elec. Servs., Inc. v. Newton, 431 Mass 763, 769 (2000). In the present case, a hearing officer in the Office of the Attorney General concluded that the omission of information from the bidding materials describing the estimated project value left bidders without the ability to determine from the bidding materials whether they satisfied the requirements for project prequalification. Accordingly, the hearing officer concluded, the bidding materials did not serve the objective of the public bidding statute "to create an open and honest competition with all bidders on an equal footing." Petricca, supra at 396. Though the decision by the hearing officer is not binding on SERSG, SERSG found it persuasive, and determined to reject all bids and reopen the process. In the circumstances, the Superior Court judge concluded that the rationale articulated by the hearing officer furnished a legitimate basis for SERSG's action, or at least did not constitute an abuse of the discretion afforded SERSG under the statute. In turn, I do not consider the judge's decision to constitute an abuse of her discretion. Contrary to the assertion by the plaintiff, the hearing officer's decision does not impose an affirmative obligation on public contracting authorities generally to include information concerning estimated project value (or, for that matter, any other specific information required to allow interested bidders to determine whether they satisfy the criteria for prequalification for a particular bid) in the bidding materials for all public contracts, beyond the specific information set forth in G. L. c. 149, § 44J. Instead, the hearing officer, and SERSG, simply concluded that in the circumstances of the present case, it would serve the public interest to reopen the bidding process because at least one interested and otherwise qualified bidder was disqualified by reason of information of which it was unaware that was not included in the bidding materials, and could easily have rectified the shortcoming had the information been available before it submitted its bid. Petition denied. (Green, J.). *Notice/Attest/Dupuis, J. Footnotes: 1. In L.L. v. [A] judge's discretionary decision constitutes an abuse of discretion where we conclude the judge made 'a clear error of judgment in weighing' the factors relevant to the decision . . . such that the decision falls outside the range of reasonable alternatives" (citations omitted.
