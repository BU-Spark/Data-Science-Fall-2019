    03/29/2016 #23 RESCRIPT (Full Opinion): The Drummer Boy Homes Association may file successive legal actions against the Brittons under G. L. c. 183A, § 6, to establish and enforce multiple contemporaneous liens on their condominium unit, each with a six-month period of priority over the first mortgage, for the recoupment of successive periods of unpaid common expenses. Accordingly, the judgment of the Appellate Division of the District Court is reversed. (By the Court)

    06/14/2016 #28 ORDER: . . . The attorney's fees assessed to the association are as follows. First, the fees represent 206.1 hours of work by one partner, Thomas O. Moriarty, at an hourly billing rate of between $385 and $410, for a total of $80,167. Second, the fees represent 27.3 hours of work by Barnett, another partner, at an hourly billing rate of between $275 and $295, for a total of $7,623. Third, the fees represent 95.5 hours of work by three associates, at an hourly billing rate of between $175 and $240, for a total of $19,798. With respect to costs, the association's counsel used the services of Bateman & Slade, Inc., a printing company, to compile the table of cases, statutes, and authorities cited in the association's brief, and to produce no less than six copies of the brief and supplemental appendix. Bateman & Slade charged the association $12,443.01 for its services. In addition, the association incurred $126.65 in out-of-pocket expenses.[4] The association's counsel state that the Brittons' filings in this litigation necessitated the preparation of certain motions that otherwise would not have been filed in appellate proceedings, resulting in additional fees and costs for the association. More specifically, the fees were described and apportioned as follows: 14.4 hours ($4,599.50) for attention to various issues and strategies; 55.3 hours ($21,290) for preparation of the application for further appellate review; 12.2 hours ($4,661) for solicitation of amicus support; 7.3 hours ($2,993) for preparation of a response to an amicus brief filed by the Federal Housing Finance Agency; 0.2 hours ($82) for research; 24.5 hours ($10,045) for preparation for oral argument; 5.7 hours ($2,337) for attendance at oral argument; 1.8 hours ($703) for client communications; and 84.7 hours ($33,456.50) for work on a brief. [2] More specifically, the fees were described and apportioned as follows: 0.7 hours ($192.5) for attention to various issues and strategies; 2.3 hours ($632) for solicitation of amicus support; 1.3 hours ($357.5) for preparation of a motion to file a new brief; 17.2 hours ($4,730) for work on a brief, appendix, and table of contents; 3.1 hours ($914.5) for attendance at oral argument; and 2.7 hours ($796.5) for preparation of a petition for attorney's fees and costs. More specifically, the fees were described and apportioned as follows: 78.4 hours ($16,434.5) for research; 0.6 hours ($135) for attention to various issues on appeal; 0.5 hours ($107.5) for assistance with preparation of the application for further appellate review; 6.3 hours ($1,417.5) for assistance with work on a brief; 0.6 hours ($111) for communications with the court; and 9.1 hours ($1,592.5) for preparation of a response in opposition to the Federal Housing Finance Agency's motion to participate in oral argument. The out-of-pocket expenses consisted of a courier fee for the filing of a motion in this court, and parking fees on the day of oral argument before this court. The association's counsel point out that the association is only obligated to pay those attorney's fees and costs that are collected from the Brittons . . . . we conclude that the number of hours billed by the association's counsel in connection with the proceedings before this court are unreasonable and excessive, except as noted, and they are modified as follows: 1. Application for further appellate review -- the request for 55.8 hours ($21,397.50) is reduced to 15 hours ($5,882.50). 2. Motion to file new brief -- the request for 1.3 hours ($357.50) remains the same. 3. New brief -- the request for 108.2 hours ($39,604) is reduced to 25 hours ($9,225). 4. Reply brief to respond to amicus brief from Federal Housing Finance Agency and others -- the request for 7.3 hours ($2,993) remains the same. Cf. Ramon v. County of Santa Clara, 173 Cal. App. 4th 915 (2009). 5. Opposition to motion filed by Federal Housing Finance Agency to participate in oral argument -- the request for 9.1 hours ($1,592.50) is denied. 6. Preparation for and performance of oral argument -- the request for 33.3 hours ($13,296.5) is reduced to 13 hours ($4,985). 7. Petition for appellate attorney's fees and costs -- the request for 2.7 hours ($796.50) remains the same. 8. Attention to various issues and strategies -- the request for 15.7 hours ($4,927) is reduced to 8 hours ($3,080). 9. Solicitation of amicus support -- the request for 14.5 hours ($5,293) is denied. 10. Research -- the request for 78.6 hours ($16,516.5) is reduced to 10 hours ($2,070). 11. Communications with the client -- the request for 1.8 hours ($703) remains the same. 12. Communications with the court -- the request for 0.6 hours ($111) remains the same. With regard to costs, we conclude that the charge of $12,443.01 from Bateman & Slade for the compilation of the table of cases, statutes, and authorities cited in the association's brief, and for the production of no less than six copies of the brief and supplemental appendix, is excessive. Accordingly, we reduce this charge to $3,000. Finally, the charge of $126.65 for out-of-pocket expenses, see note 10, supra, is reasonable. The petition for appellate attorney's fees and costs filed by the association's counsel is allowed in an amount that reflects reasonable hours expended on the association's successful appeal before this court. Counsel for the association are awarded the sum of $30,203.50 in attorney's fees, and $3,126.65 in costs. By the Court (The full and complete version of the order is on file with the clerk's office.)
