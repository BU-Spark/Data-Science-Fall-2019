    02/21/2018 #28 ORDER: (RE#26) This appeal comes to us after a remand we ordered in Hue v. Soderstrom, No. 2013-P-1683, 87 Mass. App. Ct. 1120 (unpublished memorandum and order under Rule 1:28) (May 13, 2015). In a unanimous decision, we affirmed in part and reversed in part, ruling partly in favor of appellant and partly in favor of appellee. I served on the panel in that case along with Justices Sullivan and Brown. I have been assigned to the panel hearing this appeal, along with Justices Meade and Neyman. The appellant has filed a motion asking that I recuse myself. He argues that "there is an appearance of impropriety in Justice Rubin reviewing the consequences of a decision he was a part of in the previous case. Multiple and repeated studies of the effects of confirmation bias indicate that, however unconscious, there will be an inclination for Justice Rubin to rule in a way consistent with and supportive of his earlier decision." He also asserts that "[t]he appellant does not believe that the panel as currently constituted allows for a neutral review of the facts and circumstances of his case. Simply put, he is not comfortable with Justice Rubin reviewing his case in this matter." The question of disqualification is ordinarily committed to the individual judge's sound discretion. See, e.g., Demoulas v. Demoulas Super Mkts., Inc., 428 Mass. 543, 546 (1998). In this appeal and the previous one, the members of each panel were chosen at random consistent with the requirement of statute. See G.L. c. 211A, § 3 ("The chief justice, insofar as practicable, shall assign justices to panels in such manner that each justice shall sit a substantially equal number of times with each other justice"). As a matter of basic fairness, a party does not get to select which Justices he or she would prefer to have sit on his or her case, nor does a party have the power to veto one or another Justice he or she might not prefer. A motion to recuse, however, will be allowed "in any proceeding in which the judge cannot be impartial or the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned." Massachusetts Code of Judicial Conduct Rule 2.11(A). As the comment to the Rule spells out "A judge is disqualified from any matter if the judge cannot satisfy both a subjective and an objective standard. The subjective standard requires disqualification if the judge concludes that he or she cannot be impartial. The objective standard requires disqualification whenever the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned by a fully-informed disinterested observer. . . " Rule 2.11 comment 1. There is a substantial amount of teaching from the Supreme Judicial Court about the meaning of these standards. That Court has explained that before a judge will be required to recuse himself, a litigant "ordinarily must show that the judge demonstrated a bias or prejudice arising from an extrajudicial source, and not from something learned from participation in the case." Commonwealth v. Adkinson, 442 Mass. 410, 415, 813 N.E.2d 506 (2004) (emphasis added). Indeed, "not subject to deprecatory characterization as 'bias' or 'prejudice' are opinions held by judges as a result of what they learned in earlier proceedings." Id., citing Liteky v. United States, 510 U.S. 540, 551 (1994). I know nothing about this matter other than what I learned through sitting on the case when it was previously before the court. And, although the appellant suggests that there is some appearance of impropriety in my hearing this case after a remand ordered by a panel of this court of which I was a member, all appellate judges routinely hear matters after remand whether or not they sat on the panel that sat on the original appeal. Indeed, because the Supreme Judicial Court always sits en banc, virtually every case it hears after remand will be heard by judges who sat on the panel that ordered the remand. Because there is nothing improper about it, the Supreme Judicial Court has said clearly that even a "judge who has ruled against a litigant is not thereby barred from participating in subsequent proceedings involving that litigant." Erickson v. Commonwealth, 462 Mass. 1006, 1007 n.1 (2012). I have undertaken the two-step inquiry required of judges faced with a motion to recuse, see Lena v. Commonwealth, 369 Mass. 571, 575 (1976). Having consulted my own "emotions and conscience," id., I have concluded that I do not lack the capacity to act fairly and impartially in this matter. I have also undertaken an objective appraisal of whether this is a proceeding in which my impartiality might reasonably be questioned, id., and have determined it is not. Consequently, the appellant's motion is denied. (Rubin, J.). Notice
