    08/07/2017 #4 ORDER: The petition is denied. As to likelihood of success on the merits, the town's skepticism is understandable as to whether the plaintiff's interaction with the dog caused the plaintiff any injury or aggravated his pre-existing condition. However, the only item in the record that arguably directly contradicts the plaintiff's account appears to be the hearsay statement by the female dog owner, contained in the report of the administrative lieutenant, to the effect that she observed no problem with the dog and any officer at the scene. Also, although it is possible (as the town suggests) that the independent medical examiner was not fully informed as to the plaintiff's medical history, the fact remains that his report identifies an apparent causal relationship between the reported incident with the dog and the plaintiff's lower back injuries. Accordingly, based on the necessarily abbreviated factual presentation at the preliminary injunction stage, I concur with the motion judge that the plaintiff has shown a likelihood of success on the merits, although, reviewing the purely documentary record de novo, I do not view the likelihood as overwhelming. I note that the town has not argued that a different standard applies to its obligation to indemnify for medical and related expenses under G.L. c. 41, § 100, than to its obligation to make payments under G.L. c. 41, § 111F, and thus for present purposes I will assume without deciding that the § 111F standard governs under § 100 as well. As to irreparable harm and the public interest, it must be recognized that payments under the statutes at issue do serve the public interest, not merely the private interest of the injured officer. As to § 100, "[i]t is plain that the statute serves the purpose of attracting efficient, competent workers" and "recognizes the obligation of the public to those employees who risk injury and death in the performance of hazardous duties in their daily work." Berube v. Selectmen of Edgartown, 336 Mass. 634, 638 (1958). The same may be said of § 111F, which, in addition, contains "uncommonly forceful language" requiring timely payment of amounts due. Todino v. Town of Wellfleet, 448 Mass. 234, 235 (2007). The efficacy of these statutes in helping to attract and retain high-quality police officers could be diminished if officers injured in the line of duty were required to exhaust all other possibilities (such as sick leave banks, or obtaining substitute health insurance through a spouse's employer, as the town suggests here) before obtaining preliminary injunctive relief requiring municipal employers to pay amounts apparently due. In short, even taking into account the financial burden on the town, the public interest reflected in the statutes may be better served by payment during the pendency of disputes like this one where a likelihood of success has been shown. The plaintiff no doubt understands that if he does not prevail on the merits, the town may seek to recover the amounts paid to him under the preliminary injunction. In addition I note that the town has not shown that additional sick leave from the bank is actually available to the plaintiff (as a majority vote of the governing body is required), or that comparable health insurance through the plaintiff's spouse's employment is actually available in timely fashion or at no greater cost than what plaintiff pays through his employment with the town. Nor has the town effectively rebutted the assertions of irreparable harm made in paragraphs 9 and 10 of the plaintiff's affidavit. And although the town is correct that financial harm ordinarily does not constitute irreparable harm, that is not invariably the case. See, e.g., Boston Teachers Union, Local 66 v. City of Boston, 382 Mass. 553, 567-568 (1981); Woods v. Executive Office of Communities and Devel., 411 Mass. 599 (1992). In sum, the motion judge did not abuse his discretion in granting the preliminary injunction. (Sacks, J.). *Notice/Attest/Moriarty, J.
