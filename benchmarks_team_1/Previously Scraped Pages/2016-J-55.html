    02/24/2016 #6 MEMORANDUM AND ORDER: In this summary process action, judgment by default entered for the plaintiff landlord on December 1, 2015. The defendant tenant moved for the removal of the default judgment, but did not show up at the scheduled hearing. After a Housing Court judge denied the tenant's motion for want of prosecution, the tenant renewed her motion. After hearing, the judge denied the renewed motion by order dated January 4, 2016, and the tenant appealed that denial. By order dated January 26, 2016, the judge set the appeal bond in the amount of $6,482.62 (calculated to cover nine months of unpaid back rent at $700 per month, and $182.62 in costs), required that the tenant pay $700 per month use and occupancy going forward, and denied her motion to waive the posting of the bond and periodic payments. Before me now as single justice is the tenant's appeal of the judge's January 26, 2016 order. See G. L. c. 239, § 5. She challenges both the judge's refusal to waive the bond and period payments, and the judge's setting of the amount of the security. Waiver of the appeal bond and periodic payments. The tenant has demonstrated that she is indigent.[1] Therefore, whether the appeal bond/periodic payments should be waived turns on whether the tenant has demonstrated "nonfrivolous" issues on appeal.[2] Home Sav. Bank of America v. Camillo, 45 Mass. App. Ct. 910, 911 (1998) (finding of frivolousness "requires more than the judge's conclusion that the defense is not a winner"). In the current posture of this case, the tenant has to show a nonfrivolous defense to the summary process action, a nonfrivolous reason for missing two scheduled court dates, and a timely appeal. I need not consider the first two hurdles, because her appeal appears to founder on the third. A summary process judgment must be appealed within ten days, and that deadline is jurisdictional. Jones v. Manns, 33 Mass. App. Ct. 485, 488-489 (1992). Here the judgment was issued on December 1, 2015, and the only appeal filed was on January 15, 2016. Even if the filing of the motion to remove the default and the renewal of that motion (after it was denied) were seen as tolling the appeal period, the tenant did not appeal the denial of the motion within ten days.[3] See Manzaro v. McCann, 401 Mass. 880, 882 (1988) (in summary process actions, "when a timely [postjudgment] motion . . . is filed, the time for appeal for all parties shall run from the entry of the order granting or denying that motion"). Because the only underlying appeal would not appear to have been timely brought, I cannot consider the tenant's appeal as raising a nonfrivolous issue. Amount of the appeal bond/periodic payments. My review of the setting of the amount of security is de novo. The tenant has alleged a number of serious problems with the apartment that, if true, would substantially affect its habitability. These include: missing pieces from the floor in the kitchen, electrical wiring hazards in the kitchen ceiling, gaps in flooring elsewhere in the apartment, a missing sink in the bathroom, leaks from a sewer pipe and shower in the bathroom, a lack of heat in the bedrooms, and the possible presence of lead paint. The landlord does not admit such problems, although he admits a lack of knowledge of current conditions.[4] However, it does appear uncontested that the apartment has lacked a sink in its bathroom since the tenant moved in. [5] The absence of any bathroom sink is a significant factor affecting the apartment's habitability. See 105 Code Mass. Regs. § 410.150(A)(2) ("The owner shall provide . . . [a] wash basin in the same room as the toilet . . . . [for which] [t]he kitchen sink may not be substituted"). Based on the existence of this uncontested problem and giving the tenant the benefit of the doubt that some additional problems exist in the apartment, I reduce the appeal bond to $3,682.62 (a 50% reduction of ten months of unpaid back rent, plus costs). The appeal bond is to cover the period through February 2016.[6] Going forward, that is, for the month beginning March 1, 2016, I do not reduce the use and occupancy of $700 per month. Based on the commitment the landlord has shown to address the problems in the apartment and the ongoing supervision of the housing court through the pending contempt matter and otherwise, I assume the problems substantially will be addressed by March 1, 2016. Disposition. The appeal bond is set at $3,682.62. The tenant also shall pay use and occupancy in the amount of $700 per month due and payable directly to the landlord beginning on March 1, 2016.
