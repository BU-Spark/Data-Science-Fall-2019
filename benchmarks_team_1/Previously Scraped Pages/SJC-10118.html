    02/17/2009 #18 OPPOSITION to Paper #to Defendants' Application for Appellate Attorney's Fees and Costs, filed by North American Expositions Co..
    04/29/2009 #19 ORDER (By the Court): In North Am. Expositions Co. Ltd. Partnership v. Corcoran, 452 Mass. 852 (2009), this court affirmed the Superior Court judge's decision allowing the defendants' special motion to dismiss pursuant to GL c231, s.59H, the so-called "anti-SLAPP" statute. This court affirmed as well the trial court's award of attorney's fees and costs. Appelate defense counsel has requested an award of appellate attorney's fees in the amount of $166,390 and costs in the amount of $1,305.05. In accordance with the procedures set forth in Fabre v. Walton, 441 Mass. 9, 10 (2004), lead counsel filed with his motion an affidavit setting forth his and his colleagues' hourly rates and professional qualifications, as well as a detailed listing of the number of hours billed on the appeal. The plaintiffs filed an opposition, claiming that the motion was facially invalid and that the amount sought was excessive. The plaintiffs assert that this court should "simply reject" the entire fee award because it is "facially defective." In addition, they claim that the defendants are not entitled to any fees for the time spent on the briefs and oral argument before the Appeals Court because the defendants did not prevail at that level. Because the plaintiffs state no reason to support their claim that the application is facially defective and therefore that all fees should be denied, this court rejects that contention. The plaintiffs' claim that the defendants should receive no fees for work performed in the Appeals Court stage is unavailing; filing of the appeal in that court is part of the appellate process that the defendants could not avoid, and the defendants ultimately prevailed on all of their claims before this court. Where a special motion to dismiss under the anti-SLAPP statute is allowed, the award of reasonable attorney's fees and costs, including appellate fees and costs, is mandatory. See McLarnon v. Jokisch, 431 Mass. 431 Mass. 343, 350 (2000); MacDonald v. Paton, 57 Mass. App. Ct. 290, 296 (2003). Fees are not limited to activities undertaken solely in relation to the special motion. See Office One, Inc. v. Lopez, 437 Mass. 113, 126-127 (2002). In awarding attorney's fees to the defendants for proceedings in the Superior Court, the motion judge found that "the hourly rates charged were well within the range of such rates for attorneys in Boston with the skills and reputations of those involved here: $400 per hour for Robert E. McLaughlin; $275 per hour for Michael Eby; and $200 per hour for Richard D. Vetstein." The supporting affidavit for appellate fees seeks rates of $400-$450 per hour for Robert E. McLaughlin, $300-$325 per hour for Michael Eby, and $200 per hour for Richard D. Vetstein, and explains that the attorneys' hourly rates increased during the appeal. On appeal, the defendants' counsel billed for an additional attorney, John G. Hofmann, at $75-$225 per hour, and a law clerk, Lauren E. Picone, at $100 per hour. The plaintiffs state that "the reasonableness of these hourly rates are not contested." However, the plaintiffs are correct that the total number or hours billed is excessive. To support their fee claim of $166,390, the defendants indicate that three attorneys spent 274.2 hours on the appeal before the Appeals Court and that those attorneys plus one other spent 324.3 hours on the appeal before this court; a recent law school graduate who was awaiting admission to the bar spent an additional 5.2 hours. The fees are sought for preparation of briefs, reply briefs, and oral argument before the Appeals Court and this court. The defendants point out that the case was on this court's reserve list for May, 2008, oral argument, but was not called until October, 2008, and that during the intervening period several cases relevant to this appeal were decided which required additional preparation for oral argument. Notwithstanding the work entailed by the new cases, the total time spent on arguments which had largely been made in the Superior Court was not reasonable. Memoranda and arguments on the anti-SLAPP issue were presented in the Superior Court and the defendants were adequately compensated there for their fees relevant to the Superior Court proceedings. Nevertheless, the records indicate that three attorneys spent an additional approximately 182 hours researching and writing the Appeals Court brief and reply brief; two of those attorneys, an additional attorney, and one law clerk then spent in excess of another 200 hours researching, drafting or preparing the brief and reply brief submitted to this court. The materials for this court on the anti-SLAPP arguments consisted essentially the same arguments made to the Appeals Court and much of the argument before the Superior Court. Although a few relevant cases issued during the appellate process, the majority of the arguments advanced in both appellate proceedings were largely those made in the Superior Court. The defendants, however, billed for at least 32 hours of research on anti-SLAPP cases during the proceedings before the Appeals Court, and another at least 18 hours of similar research during the proceedings before this court. fn3 In the framework of a motion to dismiss under the anti-SLAPP statute, where many of the same arguments had been made earlier and most of the same experienced attorneys had worked on the Superior Court proceedings, the amount of time spent in preparation of the appellate briefs is well beyond the outer limits of reasonableness. fn4 See Stow v. Bologna, 417 Mass. 199, 203 (1994). The billed hours also reflect in other ways both duplication of effort and unreasonable expenditure of time. See T & D Vidio, Inc. v. Revere, 66 Mass. App. Cty. 461, 476 (2006), S.C., 450 Mass. 107 (2007). For example, one attorney replaced another after the proceedings before the Appeals Court; that attorney and an attorney familiar with the case billed more than 20 hours apparently devoted to familiarizing the new attorney with the case and with appellate procedure. See Price v. Cole, 31 Mass. App. Ct. 1, 7 (1991). Another approximately 8.5 hours were evidently devoted to reviewing rules of appellate procedure. fn5 Given experienced counsel and the hourly rates charged, such review should not have been necessary. In addition, 6 hours were apparently consumed preparing a motion to file a brief that exceeded the page limit, and another approximately 20 hours were spent on filing a motion for an extension of time. This amount of time on simple administrative matters is not reasonable. Moreover, the briefs to both appellate courts were picked up from the printer and hand-delivered to the court by an attorney; submitting documents for filing did not require the expertise of experienced legal counsel. Two attorneys also billed over 58 hours to prepare the application for further appellate review. One of these attorneys is highly experienced and should not have had to research, as the billing records indicate was done, the procedure for submitting an application for further appelate review. As with the briefs filed in both appellate courts, an attorney billed time spent to arrange printing with a printing service and personally picked up the bound documents from the printer. This expenditure of time by an attorney was not necessary. In addition, the records show that two attorneys spent over 59 hours preparing for the oral argument before the Appeals Court and 52 hours preparing for the oral argument before this court. Particularly given that the issues had been argued in the trial court, the expenditure of that kind of time cannot be justified. Accordingly, the defendants' request is allowed in an amount which reflects reasonable hours expended on their appellate ligitation. See Berman v. Linnane, 434 Mass. 301, 303 (2001)(reviewing court is not required to allow or disallow bill item by item, but may "consider the bill as a whole"). The research and writing time is reduced by half, and the time spent researching anti-SLAPP cases is further reduced by an additional 7 hours. 2 hours per motion are allowed for preparation of the motions for an extension of time and to exceed the page limit. 20 hours of time are allowed for submitting the application for further appellate review. The time for preparation for oral argument is reduced by 30 hours for the argument before the Appeals Court and by an additional 25 hours for the argument before this court. The defendants are awarded the sum of $67,400 in attorney's fees and $1,305.05 in costs. This order shall be enforced in all respects by the Suffolk Division of the Superior Court Department. This method of reporting unnecessarily complicated the process of reviewing the fee request and, in some cases, precluded a precise determination of how billed time was spent.)(fn4: Arguments were presented on some issues on appeal that were not made in the Superior Court, e.t., claims asserting a breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing and conversion of good will. These issues, however, were the same in the Appeals Court and in this court.)(fn5: Because the supporting documentation did not summarize or subtotal the fees requested in a meaningful manner, and some entries did not divide the time spent among several tasks, it is, as we have indicated, impossible to state precisely the time billed for particular activities. See Twin Fires Investment, LLC v. Morgan Stanley Dean Witter & Co., 445 Mass. 411, 428-429 (2005).) (Notice to counsel and trial court.)
