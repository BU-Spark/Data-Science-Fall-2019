    07/20/2016 #15 ORDER: This matter is remanded to the District Court for findings, to be made and filed with the Clerk for the Commonwealth within ninety (90) days of the date of this order. See Commonwealth v. Yacobian, 393 Mass. 1005 (1984); Commonwealth v. O'Neil, 233 Mass. 535, 543 (1919). The judge shall address the following questions, as well as any other questions he deems appropriate. 1. Is there a scientifically established correlation between marijuana use and impaired driving? If the answer is "no," there is no need to proceed further. If the answer is "yes," then the following questions should be addressed. 2. What physical characteristics (e.g., bloodshot eyes, dilated pupils, lack of coordination, slow balance or reaction times, garbled or slow speech) permit an inference of impaired driving by reason of marijuana use? 3. Where there is marijuana use, is there a scientifically established correlation between performance on field sobriety tests and impaired driving? 4. Is the science behind the answers to questions 2 and 3 based on theory and methodology that is generally accepted in the relevant scientific community, or that is otherwise reliable? See Commonwealth v. Lanigan, 419 Mass. 15, 25-26 (1994). 5. Are the observed physical characteristics of marijuana use as it may impair a subject's ability to drive a car, together with the observations of a subject's performance on field sobriety tests, within the common experience of a lay person such that a jury is capable of deciding, without expert testimony, whether a subject operated a motor vehicle while under the influence of marijuana? 6. Similarly, may a police officer who observed a subject's physical appearance and performance on field sobriety tests offer a lay opinion that the subject was under the influence of marijuana? 7. If expert testimony is required, is there a training program for police officers that would qualify them to offer an expert opinion? The following questions, although not necessary, may lead to useful information. 8. Do the effects of marijuana change as the amount used increases? If yes, what are the changes, and what quantity must be consumed over what period of time to bring about those changes? 9. How does a person's physiology affect the extent to which the characteristics of marijuana use reflect an impairment in driving, if at all? 10. Does a person develop a tolerance to marijuana such that more must be consumed to experience or manifest the same effects? 11. Are there any scientific tests that can determine the level of marijuana in a person's system? If "yes," is there a level that is generally accepted as establishing impairment as to driving? Has any jurisdiction, foreign or domestic, recognized such a level? The judge is free to amend the reported questions, or report additional questions.

    09/19/2017 #28 RESCRIPT (Full Opinion): We answer the reported questions as follows: 1. "No." Police officers may not testify to the administration and results of FSTs as they do in operating under the influence of alcohol prosecutions. Police officers may testify to the administration of "roadside assessments" in the manner set forth in this opinion. 2. "No." A lay witness may not offer an opinion that another person is "high" on marijuana. 3. "Yes." A police officer may testify to observed physical characteristics of the driver such as blood shot eyes, drowsiness, and lack of coordination. The officer is not permitted to offer an opinion that these characteristics mean that the driver is under the influence of marijuana. 4. "Yes." Jurors are permitted to utilize their common sense in assessing trial evidence. The case is remanded to the District Court for further proceedings consistent with this opinion. (By the Court)
