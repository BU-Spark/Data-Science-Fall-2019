    02/03/2011 #18 ORDER: Among the issues on appeal, the Massachusetts Water Resources Authority (MWRA) claims that the jury's award of damages in the amount of $2,900,000 for the South Parcel must be reduced to $1,800,562, and asks that we vacate the judgment and remand the case for entry of judgment consistent with the lower damage award. At trial, Boston Edison Company's (BECO)expert appraiser, Robert Coleman, opined that the damages suffered from the takings on the South Parcel totaled $11,820,000, of which $1,800,562 were direct damages and the balance were damages arising from the relocation of the switch house. Because the jury found that neither the takings nor the Braintree Weymouth Relief Facilities Project caused BECO's relocation of the switch house, the MWRA argues that the evidence supported an award of damages only for the amount of direct damages stated in Coleman's opinion, not for the higher amount awarded by the jury in its answer to a special question. Under Mass. R. Civ. P. 59(a), a party may move for a new trial or, in the alternative, for a remittitur, on the grounds that the verdict was against the weight of the evidence or excessive. D'Annolfo v. Stoneham Housing Auth., 375 Mass. 650, 659 (1978). Here, the judge denied the MWRA's motion for new trial or for remittitur without setting forth his reasons. Because the denial of a motion for new trial or for remittitur rests in the sound direction of the trial judge, the denial may be reversed only for an abuse of discretion. Loschi v. Mass. Port Auth., 361 Mass. 714, 715 (1972) (Loschi). An abuse of discretion will be found "only where the damages awarded were 'greatly disproportionate to the injury proved,'" id., quoting Haven v. Brimfield, 345 Mass. 529, 534 (1963), "or where 'it appears to the judicial conscience . . . that otherwise a miscarriage of justice will result.'" Loschi, supra, quoting Bartley v. Phillips, 317 Mass. 35, 41 (1944). We have not established a rule that a damage award in an eminent domain case may not be more than the highest opinion of value in evidence, or lower than the lowest opinion of value. Loschi, supra at 716 (affirming jury award greater than fair market value claimed by owner); Appelstein v. Boston Redevelopment Auth., 359 Mass. 746 (1971) (affirming jury award less than defendant expert's opinion of value). "The jury may use their general knowledge and experience in evaluating property and are not required to follow blindly the opinions of experts." Loschi, supra. However, where we have affirmed jury awards in eminent domain cases that are more than the highest opinion of value or less than the lowest opinion, we have done so based on "meaningful and significant evidence other than the opinions of value" that supported the jury's award. Id. See also Appelstein v. Boston Redevelopment Auth., supra. Before we resolve this issue on appeal, we wish to know the judge's reasons for denying the MWRA's motion for new trial or, in the alternative, for remittitur as to the damage award regarding the South Parcel and what evidence, if any, he relied upon in denying the motion. We therefore direct the judge to provide this court with a statement explaining his reasons and identifying the relevant evidence, no later than thirty days from the date of this order. The judge is also free, if he wishes, to reconsider his denial of the motion and to make any different or additional rulings that he deems appropriate at this time. We shall retain jurisdiction of this case pending receipt of the statement of reasons (or further rulings) from the judge. The parties will then have an opportunity to provide supplemental memoranda addressing the new matter before we proceed to decide the case..
