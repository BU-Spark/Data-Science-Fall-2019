    12/27/2017 #8 MEMORANDUM AND ORDER OF DISMISSAL: The plaintiff appeals from a Superior Court judgment dismissing his complaint for judicial review of a decision of the zoning board of appeals of Dudley (zoning board). That decision had affirmed the enforcement decision of the Dudley building inspector requiring the plaintiff to remove a mobile home trailer from his property. The plaintiff sought judicial review of the zoning board's decision pursuant to both G. L.c. 40A, § 17, and G. L. c. 249, § 4. The zoning board moved to dismiss the complaint pursuant to Mass.R.Civ.P. 12(b)(6), 365 Mass. 754 (1974), on the ground that the plaintiff's appeal to the zoning board was untimely and the appeal to the Superior Court was defective because the plaintiff failed to attach a copy of the board's decision to his complaint as required by G. L. c. 40A, § 17. The motion was allowed, and a judgment dismissing the plaintiff's complaint was entered on the trial court docket on April 27, 2016. Although the notice of the action does not appear to have been mailed to the parties until May 5, 2016, the notice of judgment clearly indicates the judgment had entered on April 27, 2016. The notice of appeal was served on May 27, 2016, but was not received and docketed by the Superior Court until June 3, 2016. The notice of appeal, filed more than thirty days after the judgment entered, is untimely. See Mass.R.A.P. 4(a), as amended, 464 Mass. 160 (2013); Aroesty v. Cohen, 62 Mass. App. Ct. 215, 217-218 & n.4 (2004) (neither date judgment was rendered nor date notice was sent is relevant; time for filing notice runs from date judgment is entered on the docket). See also Standard Register Co. v. Bolton-Emerson, Inc., 35 Mass. App. Ct. 570, 571-572 (1993) (time for filing notice of appeal runs from date of entry of judgment on docket). Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed as untimely. Even were the appeal properly before us, the plaintiff would fare no better. The building inspector's enforcement letter was issued to the plaintiff on September 25, 2014. Although the amended complaint alleges the plaintiff "made a timely appeal of this decision by correspondence to the Building Inspector," and thereafter "requested a hearing before the zoning board for months," the amended complaint does not allege, even after the procedural defect was noted in the zoning board's motion to dismiss, that the plaintiff complied with the applicable appeal provisions of G. L. c. 40A, § 15.[1] The only indication of an appeal by the plaintiff in the record is his application for a hearing before the board which was not filed until August 13, 2015, almost a year after the building inspector's enforcement decision.[2] Because the appeal was not commenced by the plaintiff within the statutory thirty-day appeal period, it was untimely, "thereby depriving the zoning board and the Superior Court of jurisdiction to entertain [the] appeal." Miles-Matthias v. Zoning Bd. of Appeals of Seekonk, 84 Mass. App. Ct. 778, 783-784 (2014). As the zoning board lacked jurisdiction to consider the plaintiff's untimely appeal, its consideration of the merits of his claim cannot constitute a waiver. The case cited by the plaintiff, Worcester County Christian Communications, Inc. v. Board of Appeals of Spencer, 22 Mass. App. Ct. 83, 84 (1986), is distinguishable and would not compel a different result. In that case, the town board's lack of jurisdiction was created by the inaction of a town official (the failure of a building inspector to act on an application for a permit), not the failure of a party to file an appeal in accordance with a required statutory framework. See Warrington v. Zoning Bd. of Appeals of Rutland, 78 Mass. App. Ct. 903, 905 & n.4 (2010). Given our result, we need not reach the issue whether, in these circumstances, the failure to attach a "certified copy" of the decision to the complaint rendered the appeal fatally defective. So much of the complaint as sought certiorari relief pursuant to G. L. c. 249, § 4, was also properly dismissed as the Legislature has provided an avenue for appeal and judicial review of the zoning board decision. See Cumberland Farms, Inc. v. Planning Bd. of Bourne, 56 Mass. App. Ct. 605, 607-608 (2002). Appeal dismissed. (Agnes, Blake & McDonough, JJ.[3]) *Notice/attest/Kenton-Walker, J. Footnotes [1] The section, as appearing in St. 1987, c. 498, § 3, provides: "Any appeal under section eight to a permit granting authority shall be taken within thirty days from the date of the order or decision which is being appealed. The petitioner shall file a notice of appeal specifying the grounds thereof, with the city or town clerk, and a copy of said notice, including the date and time of filing certified by the town clerk, shall be filed forthwith by the petitioner with the officer or board whose order or decision is being appealed, and to the permit granting authority, specifying in the notice grounds for such appeal." [2] The plaintiff cannot rely on an argument that there are disputed issues of fact concerning whether his appeal was timely, when his amended complaint fails to allege his compliance with the statutory requirements for an appeal, even after the deficiencies were previously noted in the zoning board's motion to dismiss. [3] The panelists are listed in order of seniority.
