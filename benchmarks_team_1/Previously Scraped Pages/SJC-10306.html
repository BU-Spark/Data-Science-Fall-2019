    11/26/2008 #3 ORDER (By the Court): Thomas Hendricks pleaded guilty in the Superior Court to certain offenses in 2005 and was sentenced to a term in prison. He maintains that he is entitled to thirteen months' credit for time served awaiting trial. His motions in the trial court to correct the mittimus to reflect this contention were unsuccessful. In April, 2008, he also filed a motion for resentencintg, purportedly pursuant to MRCrimP 30(a), and a motion to review and revoke his sentence, pursuant to MRCrimP 29(a); a judge in the Superior Court ruled that no action was necessary on these motions. Hendricks's appeal from these rulings is pending in the Appeals Court. Hendricks moved in the Appeals Court for a stay of his sentence pending appeal. (f.n.1 - Hendricks did not first request a stay from the Superior Court judge.... He argues that to do so would have been futile. We need not resolve that contention at this juncture.) A single justice of the Appeals Court denied the motion, citing Comm. v. Hodge (No. 1), 380 Mass. 851 (1980). Hendricks thereafter filed a motion for a stay of his sentence in the county court. (f.n.2 - His motion also included a cursory alternative request for a writ of habeas corpus. In its opposition to the motion, the Commonwealth observed that it did not appear from the certificate of service that the motion had been served on the superintendent of the correctional facility. Hendricks thereafter filed a new application for a writ of habeas corpus, in which he made the same claim concerning the amount of "time served" credit to which he is entitled. A second single justice has ordered that case transferred to the Superior Court where it is now pending. That matter is not before us.) Treating the motion as a request for relief under GLc 211, s.3, a single justice of this court denied relief without a hearing. This appeal followed. Hendricks has filed a memorandum pursuant to SJC Rule 2:21.... That rule applies only "(w)hen a single justice denies relief from a challenged interlocutory ruling in the trial court."....The matter before us, a request for a stay, was never ruled on in the trial court (see note 1, supra). Moreover, the only relevant rulings in the trial court -- the Superior Court judge's rulings on Hendricks's motions for resentencing and to revise and revoke -- were not interlocutory, but final. Rule 2:21 therefore does not apply. Hendricks may thus proceed with his appeal in the regular course. In light of the time-sensitive nature of the matter, the parties shall work out a briefing schedule with the clerk of this court which assures that the case will be fully briefed by December 26, 2008. The case will be heard at the court's January sitting. (Notice to counsel.)
