    10/28/2016 #5 Memorandum of Decision & Judgment: ... "The Commonwealth is correct that it is an error of law to grant a continuance where "[t]here is nothing to suggest that the judge granted [the] continuance for any other reason than to impede the Commonwealth's prosecution of the case." Commonwealth v. Taylor, 428 Mass. 623, 628 (1999). However, unlike Taylor, there are facts here indicating the continuance has nothing to do with impeding the Commonwealth's prosecution of its case. Because the defendant's student visa was revoked and she returned to China, it remains unclear when, or if, she will be permitted to return to the United States. The continuance in this case provides a limited opportunity for the defendant to resolve her visa issues and return to this county in the future to resolve this criminal matter; the continuance was appropriate to insure that the interests of justice were served. See Mass R. Crim. P. 10 (a) (1). The Commonwealth also argues that the continuance is an impermissible disposition of an OUI case. See Commonwealth v. Quispe, 433 Mass. 508, 511-512 (2001). However, the continuance was no "disposition" at all. The continuance here does not resolve the case; it merely gives the defendant time to sort out her visa situation before the OUI charge returns to the trial list in July 2017. Finally, the Boston Municipal Court judge, through counsel, argues that the Commonwealth improperly named him as a respondent in this matter. Generally, "where a plaintiff questions the action of a judge in his official capacity," it is "more appropriate" to name the court on which the judge serves rather than the individual judge. Fadden v. Commonwealth, 376 Mass. 604, 609 (1978). Applying that principle here, the Boston Municipal Court should be substituted as the respondent in the place of the judge. ORDER, The Commonwealth's petition for relief under G. L. c. 211, § 3, is DENIED." (Botsford, J.)
