    04/20/2017 #7 MEMORANDUM AND ORDER The plaintiff, an electrical contractor, brought a complaint against the defendants seeking damages of $37,500 for breach of contract to pay for goods and services sold and delivered to Universal Quality Machine, LLC (Universal) or its predecessor under a contract signed on Universal's behalf by its general manager, David Moore.[2] The plaintiff also alleged that Moore had misrepresented that Universal had sufficient funds to pay for the goods and services to be provided, when he knew that Universal was seriously undercapitalized, in debt, and had no funds. Moore now purports to appeal from the default judgment entered against him under Mass.R.Civ.P. 55(b)(1), as amended, 454 Mass. 1401 (2009), for the sum certain of $37,500, plus interest and costs. So far as appears on a minimal record, no final judgment adjudicating all claims entered below.[3] Accordingly, we dismiss the appeal, but state our views by way of dictum. Moore admits that he was served with the complaint on or before April 23, 2014. The plaintiff filed its first amended complaint on April 25, 2014, and its second amended complaint on November 13, 2014. Moore was defaulted on March 27, 2015, and on April 24, 2015, a default judgment entered against him under rule 55(b)(1), for the sum certain of $37,500, plus interest and costs. On June 24, 2015, Moore filed a motion seeking relief from the default judgment under Mass.R.Civ.P. 60(a), 365 Mass. 828 (1974), on grounds of clerical mistake, or alternatively under Mass.R.Civ.P. 60(b)(1), 365 Mass. 828 (1974), on grounds of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, claiming that he had not been served with either the first or the second amended complaints.[4] After hearing, a Superior Court judge denied Moore's motion, concluding: "The amended complaints assert no new or additional claims against this defendant. Mass.R.Civ.P. 5(1) excuses a party from serving pleadings or other documents 'on any party in default for failure to appear except that any pleading asserting new or additional claims for relief against him shall be served upon him in the manner provided for service of summons in [r]ule 4.' Wang v. Niakaros, 67 Mass. App. Ct. 166, 170 (2006)." On April 21, 2016, almost one year after the entry of the default judgment against him, Moore filed a second motion for relief from judgment under rule 60(b)(1), this time supported by affidavit. Treating this motion as one for reconsideration, a second Superior Court judge denied it, noting correctly that "[t]he defendant appears to raise no issues different from those raised previously." This appeal followed. In this court, Moore has failed to reproduce the initial complaint, thereby preventing any review of the pleadings or the first judge's assessment that there were no new or additional claims against him.[5] Even assuming that by the most recent motion he raised sufficient grounds to establish excusable neglect,[6] which he has not, he failed to raise the issue sufficiently in his initial motion, from which he did not appeal. He may not use the most recent motion as a substitute for the appeal process. See Muir v. Hall, 37 Mass. App. Ct. 38, 41 (1994). Were we to reach the merits, however, based on the limited record before us, we would agree with the first judge's cogent analysis. Appeal dismissed. (Vuono, Meade & Maldonado, JJ.[7]).
