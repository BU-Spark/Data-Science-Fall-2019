    02/02/2012 #7 ORDER: Appeal allowed to proceed. The Commonwealth appeals from a judgment of a single justice of this court denying its petition for relief under G. L. c. 211, § 3. The matter arises from a case in the Superior Court in which Jessie Jordan is charged with assault with intent to murder and other offenses. On a motion filed by Jordan, a judge ordered the Commonwealth, among other things, to ask the alleged victim whether he was an informant for a law enforcement agency and to report the answer to Jordan's counsel. The Commonwealth seeks relief from this order. The case is now before this court pursuant to S.J.C. Rule 2:21, as amended, 434 Mass. 1301 (2001). Because the underlying Superior Court order is interlocutory, the Commonwealth is required by the rule to "set forth the reasons why review of the trial court decision cannot adequately be obtained on appeal from any final adverse judgment in the trial court or by other available means." Rule 2:21 (2). That is the single, very limited focus of the rule. On that limited point, we agree with the Commonwealth that it cannot adequately obtain appellate review of the order at issue by other means. Cf. Villalta v. Commonwealth, 428 Mass. 429, 432-33 (1998), and cases cited. The appeal thus satisfies the Rule 2:21 standard and may therefore proceed in the ordinary course. The Commonwealth should be mindful, however, as it pursues this appeal, that the fact that it has no other remedy does not automatically entitle it to consideration of the substantive merits of its claim. See Commonwealth v. Richardson, 454 Mass. 1005, 1005-1006 (2009); Commonwealth v. Cook, 380 Mass. 314, 319 (1980). It will first be incumbent on the Commonwealth to demonstrate that this is the type of "rare case" and "exceptional circumstance" that requires the exercise of the court's extraordinary power of general superintendence; more specifically, that the single justice, who denied the petition summarily, without a hearing, and without addressing the merits, abused her discretion in declining to employ the court's extraordinary superintendence power in these circumstances. See Commonwealth v. Barros, 460 Mass. 1015, 1016 (2011); Commonwealth v. Richardson, supra. This being an appeal from the single justice's decision, and not a de novo review of the petition or second bite at the apple, it will not be enough for the Commonwealth simply to repeat the same arguments to this court that it pressed unsuccessfully before the single justice. Commonwealth v. Samuels, 456 Mass. 1025, 1027 n. 1 (2010). See Commonwealth v. Barros, supra at 1017. The full court will reach the substantive merits only if it first determines that the case presents the type of extraordinary situation requiring consideration of the merits under G. L. c. 211, § 3, and that the single justice abused her discretion in ruling otherwise. Notice sent.
