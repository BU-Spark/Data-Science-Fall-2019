    05/25/2017 #18 ORDER OF DISMISSAL In this post-foreclosure eviction action, a Housing Court judge allowed summary judgment for possession of real property in favor of the plaintiff, Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. Among other things, the judge concluded that the defendant, Ronald L. Morse, lacked standing to challenge the assignment of the mortgage to Wells Fargo. Morse challenges that ruling on appeal. Because we conclude that Morse's notice of appeal was not timely filed, the appeal is hereby dismissed. The subject real property is a two-family home located at 9 Dodge Street in Beverly (the property). (R.A. at 133; 338 at Para. 2) Morse obtained title to the property by deed dated December 17, 1996, and recorded on December 18, 1996. (R.A. at 342) Ten years later, in December 2006, Morse borrowed $194,700 from Fremont Investment & Loan ("Fremont"), and secured that debt with a mortgage on the property, granted to Mortgage Electronic Registration Systems, Inc. ("MERS") as nominee for Fremont. (R.A. at 118-120) In January, 2012, MERS assigned the mortgage to Wells Fargo, which was recorded in the Registry of Deeds on March 29, 2012. (R.A. at 115-116) Thereafter, on September 20, 2013, Wells Fargo conducted a foreclosure sale and purchased the property for $230,030. (R.A. at 106) A foreclosure deed and affidavit of sale were recorded in the Registry of Deeds on April 8, 2014. (R.A. at 105-107) When Morse did not vacate the property, Wells Fargo served him with a notice to quit dated May 9, 2014. (R.A. at 59) Morse refused to leave and Wells Fargo commenced the underlying eviction action in the Northeast Housing Court. (R.A. at 7-8) After Morse answered and asserted counterclaims, (R.A. at 9-23) Wells Fargo moved for summary judgment seeking a judgment for possession in its favor and dismissal of Morse's counterclaims. (R.A. at 3; 93-96) On November 23, 2015, a Housing Court judge allowed summary judgment in favor of Wells Fargo. (R.A. at 4; 370) Final judgment entered on November 25, 2015. (R.A. at 4) Morse filed his notice of appeal fourteen days later, on December 9, 2015. (R.A. at 4) The time period for appeal from a summary process judgment is ten days. See G. L. c. 239, § 5. The ten-day period for appealing from summary process cases is "fixed by statute and is jurisdictional." Jones v. Manns, 33 Mass. App. Ct. 485, 488-89 (1992); G. L. c. 239, § 5(a). Because the tenth day after judgment entered was a Saturday, the deadline for the notice of appeal was December 7, 2015. Mass.R.Civ.P. 6(a). Morse's notice of appeal filed on December 9, 2015, was not timely and the appeal is dismissed.[1] (Kinder, Henry & Desmond, JJ.[2]). Notice/attest/Kerman, J. Footnotes: [1] We note that Morse would not prevail even if we were to reach the merits as he lacks standing to challenge the assignment of the mortgage. See U.S. Bank National Association v. Bolling, 90 Mass. App. Ct. 154, 156-157 (2016). [2] The panelists are listed in order of seniority.
