    09/16/2011 #27 ORDER (By the Court): In Psy-Ed Corporation v. Klein (No. 1), 459 Mass. 697 (2011), an employment discrimination case, this court affirmed the finding of the trial judge in favor of the plaintiff Kimberly Schive that the defendants violated G. L. c. 151B, § 4 (4) and (4A), when they retaliated against her for bringing her employment discrimination claim to the Massachusetts Commission Against Discrimination. Id. at 710-712. The court also affirmed the award of damages in the amount of $125,000. Id. at 721. Following up on a request she made in her appellate brief, Schive now seeks to recover her attorney's fees for the legal work performed on her behalf during the appellate proceedings; she seeks to do so against the defendant Psy-Ed Corporation, and not against the individual defendant Joseph Valenzano, Jr. As the prevailing party in her claim of retaliation for legally protected conduct in violation of G. L. c. 151B, § 4 (4) and 4 (4A), Schive is entitled to an award of "reasonable" appellate attorney's fees pursuant to G. L. c. 151B, § 9. See DeRoche v. Massachusetts Comm'n Against Discrimination, 447 Mass. 1, 18 (2006), citing Lowell v. Massachusetts Comm'n Against Discrimination, 65 Mass. App. Ct. 356, 357 (2006). See also Haddad v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. (No. 2), 455 Mass. 1024, 1025 (2010) (Haddad (No. 2)). Schive's appellate counsel, Dahlia Rudavsky, has requested an award of $89,169.64 in fees and costs: $84,965.50 in attorney's fees for 155.9 hours of her own legal work at an hourly rate of $545; $3,288 in attorney's fees for 13.7 hours of the services of an associate in Rudavsky's law firm at an hourly rate of $240; and $916.14 in costs for work on the proceedings in this court. In accordance with the procedures set forth in Fabre v. Walton, 441 Mass. 9, 10 (2004), Rudavsky filed with her fee request affidavits setting forth her professional qualifications, and, more briefly, that of her associate, Kevin Merritt, their hourly rates, and detailed listings of the number of hours expended. She also filed copies of orders issued by judges of the Massachusetts Superior Court and United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts that identify hourly rates for fees awarded to other attorneys who practice in the field of employment law and employment discrimination in particular. Further, Rudavsky referenced an affidavit that formed part of the record of this case on appeal, in which J. Owen Todd, an attorney and former Superior Court judge, set out information about comparable hourly rates for other lawyers in the Boston market as of 2006. In determining the amount of a reasonable fee, we consider "the nature of the case and the issues presented, the time and labor required, the amount of damages involved, the result obtained, the experience, reputation and ability of the attorney, the usual price charged for similar services by other attorneys in the same area, and the amount of awards in similar cases." Linthicum v. Archambault, 379 Mass. 381, 388-389 (1979). See Fontaine v. Ebtec Corp., 415 Mass. 309, 324-325 (1993). We consider also the interests that the statute in question is designed to protect and the public interest in allowing claims under that statute to proceed with competent counsel. See Stowe v. Bologna, 417 Mass. 199, 203 (1994); Stratos v. Department of Pub. Welfare, 387 Mass. 312, 322-323 (1982). These considerations generally support the appropriateness of Schive's fee request. The appeal involved an important but unresolved issue of law concerning the scope of a retaliation claim under G. L. c. 151B, that this court decided in accordance with the position argued by Schive. Further, as the supporting affidavit states, Rudavsky is highly regarded in her field and has obtained numerous successful outcomes for clients in difficult cases. The purpose of G. L. c. 151B, which is to discourage unlawful discrimination, and the require-ment that the statute be broadly construed, see G. L. c. 151B, § 9, reflect a legislative intent to encourage competent counsel to seek judicial relief for discrimination claims. See DeRoche v. Massachusetts Comm'n Against Discrimination, 447 Mass. at 18; Gasior v. Massachusetts Gen. Hosp., 446 Mass. 645, 654 (2006); Pielech v. Massasoit Greyhound, Inc., 47 Mass. App. Ct. 322, 327 (1999). See also Borne v. Haverhill Golf & Country Club, Inc., 58 Mass. App. Ct. 306, 324 n.17 (2003); Nardone v. Patrick Motor Sales, Inc., 46 Mass. App. Ct. 452, 453 (1999). Schive's counsel describes in her affidavit the difficulty employment discrimination plaintiffs face in obtaining counsel willing to represent them. As indicated, the appellate result obtained was favorable to Schive. The time reasonably expended and the reasonableness of the hourly rates charged to obtain the results achieved are the critical considerations in determining an appropriate fee award. See Haddad (No. 2), 455 Mass. at 1025-1027; Berman v. Linnane, 434 Mass. 301, 303 (2001). As for reasonable hourly rates, we examine first "the average rates in the attorney's community for similar work done by attorneys of the same years' experience." Stratos v. Department of Pub. Welfare, 387 Mass. at 323. "[W]here the award is provided for by statute and is assessed against the party having no contractual relationship with the attorney involved, the standard of reasonableness depends not on what the attorney usually charges but, rather, on what his services were objectively worth." Heller v. Silverbranch Constr. Corp., 376 Mass. 621, 629 (1978). Schive's fee application is based on hourly rates of $545 for Rudavsky and $240 for Merritt. Rudavsky has thirty-one years of litigation experience. She has served as an elected officer of multiple professional organizations, received prestigious awards, published and lectured on employment law and trial advocacy, and has been a frequent speaker at professional gatherings. Merritt has been a member of the Massachusetts bar since 2006. We conclude that in all the circumstances, an hourly rate of $400 for Rudavsky and $175 for Merritt would be reasonable. Turning to the number of hours billed, the documentation accompanying each billed line item provides sufficient detail to describe the work performed. Schive bears the burden of establishing and supporting the number of hours billed, see Twin Fires Inv., LLC v. Morgan Stanley Dean Witter & Co., 445 Mass. 411, 428 (2005), and we conclude she has met that burden. The hours and tasks appear reasonable. Costs are allowed as billed. Thus, the plaintiff Kimberly Schive is awarded the sum of $64,757.50 in attorney's fees, including $62,360 for services rendered by Rudavsky and $2,397.50 for services rendered by Merritt; and $916.14 in costs.
