    02/02/2018 #12 ORDER: On November 17, 2015, a single justice allowed the Commonwealth's application for leave to pursue an interlocutory appeal of the allowance by a Superior Court judge of the defendant's motion to suppress evidence. See Mass. R. Crim. P. 15(a)(2), as appearing in 422 Mass. 1501 (1996). On October 10, 2017, the full court issued its rescript decision vacating the allowance of the defendant's motion to suppress, and remanding the matter to the Superior Court for further proceedings. Commonwealth v. Perkins, 478 Mass. 97, 110 (2017). The defendant has timely filed a motion for attorney's fees and costs pursuant to Mass. R. Crim. P. 15(d), seeking an award of $28,088 in attorney's fees. The Commonwealth does not oppose the motion. Where the Commonwealth sought leave to pursue an interlocutory appeal, the award of attorney's fees to the defendant is mandatory. Mass. R. Crim. P. 15(d). See Commonwealth v. Rosario, 458 Mass. 1003, 1004 (2010); Commonwealth v. Ennis, 441 Mass. 718, 719-720 & n.1 (2004). The court considers only the appropriate amount of the award, following the procedures set forth in Fabre v. Walton, 441 Mass. 9, 10 (2004). See Commonwealth v. Ennis, supra at 721-722 & n.3. The court begins its analysis using the "lodestar" method of a reasonable hourly rate multiplied by a reasonable number of hours spent. See, e.g., Berman v. Linnane, 434 Mass. 301, 303 (2001). The burden lies with the defendant to show "that the claimed rate and number of hours are reasonable." Commonwealth v. Ennis, supra at 722. "The amount of a reasonable attorney's fee is largely discretionary with the judge, who is in the best position to determine how much time was reasonably spent on a case, and the fair value of the attorney's services." Id. at 723. The defendant requests an award of fees for 105.9 hours of work at a rate of $200 per hour for defense counsel Robert F. Hennessy, and 23 hours of work at a rate of $300 for defense counsel John M. Thompson. In support of his motion, the defendant submitted affidavits from his counsel detailing their experience and areas of expertise, and the 128.9 hours of work spent on preparing the opposition to the Commonwealth's interlocutory appeal. The reasonableness of an hourly rate begins with "the average rates in the attorney's community for similar work done by attorney's of the same years' experience." Stratos v. Department of Pub. Welfare, 387 Mass. 312, 313 (1982). In his affidavit, Hennessy asserts that his claimed $200-per-hour fee is reasonable "for attorneys with comparable skills, reputation, and experience." Thompson, who has been a criminal defense attorney for forty-two years, states that $300 per hour is a reasonable rate for an appellate attorney of his experience. He further states that he is familiar with fees charged by other appellate attorneys in Western Massachusetts with similar backgrounds, and that these rates are comparable. These rates are reasonable for the geographic area in which the attorneys practice and their level of experience. Turning to the hours charged, the time spent to prepare the defendant's opposition was not unreasonable given the complex nature of the appeal, and was fully documented in the defense counsels' affidavits. None of the work by the two different counsel appears to be duplicative. Therefore, upon consideration, it is ORDERED that the defendant's motion for attorneys' fees pursuant to Mass. R. Crim. P. 15(d) shall be, and hereby is, ALLOWED, in the amount of $28,088. Payment of the total award shall be divided equally between the Commonwealth and the AOTC. (By the Court)
