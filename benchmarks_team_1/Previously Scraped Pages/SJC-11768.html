    04/06/2015 #3 ORDER: Diane Saia appeals from a judgment of a single justice of this court denying, without a hearing, her petition for relief under G. L. c. 211, § 3. In 2010, Saia commenced a putative class action in the Superior Court against Bay State Gas Company (Bay State). An "Order Protecting Confidentiality of Discovery Materials" entered in that case, providing in relevant part that any documents, transcripts, and other materials containing confidential information filed with the court were to be filed in compliance with the Uniform Rules on Impoundment Procedure. Pursuant to that provision, Bay State moved for impoundment of certain materials it intended to file in support of its motion for summary judgment. A judge in the Superior Court allowed the motion as to most of the materials in question and entered an order impounding them. Bay State's motion for summary judgment was subsequently allowed and Saia's appeal therefrom is pending in the Appeals Court. [1] Shortly after filing her notice of appeal, Saia moved in the Superior Court to terminate or modify the impoundment order. That motion was denied. Saia filed a petition for review under G. L. c. 231, § 118. A single justice of the Appeals Court denied the petition. Saia's G. L. c. 211, § 3, petition for relief followed. Saia has filed a memorandum and appendix pursuant to S.J.C. Rule 2:21, as amended, 434 Mass. 1301 (2001). However, Rule 2:21 does not apply in these circumstances, as there is no "challenged interlocutory ruling in the trial court." S.J.C. Rule 2:21 (1). The decision of the single justice of the Appeals Court is not a ruling of the trial court. The underlying decision of the Superior Court judge is not an interlocutory ruling, as final judgment had entered when Saia filed the motion to terminate or modify the impoundment order. Neither the motion nor the denial disturbed the finality of the judgment. Because Rule 2:21 is inapplicable, we allow the appeal to proceed in the ordinary course. In doing so, however, we do not conclude that Saia has shown that she lacks an adequate remedy, more specifically, that the procedures set forth in the Uniform Rules on Impoundment Procedure are inadequate to afford her review of the judge's decision. It will be incumbent on Saia to make this demonstration in her brief. Moreover, even if Saia has no other remedy, this does not automatically entitle her to consideration of the substantive merits of her claim. See Commonwealth v. Richardson, 454 Mass. 1005, 1005-1006 (2009); Commonwealth v. Cook, 380 Mass. 314, 319 (1980). It will also be incumbent on her to demonstrate that this is the type of "rare case" and "exceptional circumstance" that requires the exercise of the court's extraordinary power of general superintendence. See Commonwealth v. Barros, 460 Mass. 1015, 1016 (2011); Commonwealth v. Richardson, supra. See also Commonwealth v. Elias, 463 Mass. 1015, 1016 n.2 (2012). [1] The Superior Court docket indicates that a separate and final judgment was entered pursuant to Mass. R. Civ. P. 54 (b) as to Saia's claims against Bay State. As Saia and Bay State are the only parties and Bay State apparently did not assert any counterclaims against Saia, it does not appear that there are any claims remaining for the Superior Court to adjudicate. (By the Court).
