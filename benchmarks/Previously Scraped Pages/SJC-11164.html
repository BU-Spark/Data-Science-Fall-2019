    06/26/2012 #3 ORDER: Appeal allowed to proceed. The defendants were indicted for armed robbery; armed assault with intent to rob; assault and battery with a dangerous weapon; assault with a dangerous weapon; and larceny over $250. The Commonwealth moved to compel them each to produce a buccal swab. After a hearing, and over the defendants' objections, a judge in the Superior Court allowed the motion. The order directing the defendants to produce the buccal swabs states that "easonable restraints may be employed" by the Commonwealth in obtaining the swabs (i.e., that the Commonwealth may employ reasonable force to ensure compliance with the order). Separately, the defendants sought review of the order from a single justice of this court. Eugene Bertini filed a G. L. c. 211, § 3, petition arguing that the Commonwealth had not satisfied its burden to show that the sample sought "will probably provide evidence relevant to the question of [his] guilt," see Commonwealth v. Trigones, 397 Mass. 633, 640 (1986), and that the use of physical force to extract a deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) sample where he has not yet been convicted of any crime violates his search and seizure rights under the Fourth Amendment and art. 14 of the Massachusetts Declaration of Rights. Lesli Bertini filed an application for leave to appeal pursuant to Mass. R. Crim. P. 15 (a) (2). She also argued that the Commonwealth had failed to show that the sample sought would probably provide evidence relevant to the question of her guilt. Additionally, she argued that, among other things, allowing the Commonwealth to obtain the buccal swab by force violates her rights to due process and against self-incrimination. The single justice denied the defendants' submissions, treating them together as petitions for relief pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3. The case is now before this court pursuant to S.J.C. Rule 2:21, as amended, 434 Mass. 1301 (2001). Eugene Bertini argues that G. L. c. 211, § 3, provides the only means by which review of the trial court order can be sought and that because he "faces a forced extraction of DNA prior to trial, his constitutional right to privacy and to be free from unlawful search and seizure . . . will be violated if the trial court's decision is not reviewed by this Court before proceeding in the trial court." In other words, once the buccal swabs are taken from the defendants, the harm, if there is any, cannot be remedied. This case presents important questions regarding the defendants' constitutional rights, including the proper procedure by which they may seek relief from the trial court's order, which are best addressed by full briefing and oral argument in this court. Accordingly, the defendants may proceed with their appeals. In addition to any issues the parties wish to address concerning the merits of the trial court's order, they should also address in their briefs whether, and how, a defendant may seek relief from an interlocutory trial court order such as the one issued here (e.g., compelling the production of a buccal swab by force if necessary). More specifically, the parties should address whether Mass. R. Crim. P. 15 provides an adequate means for the defendants to seek relief from the trial court order, see Matter of Lavigne, 418 Mass. 831, 833 (1994), and whether, if so, defendants who proceed under Rule 15 may appeal from a single justice's denial of a request for leave to appeal; alternatively, whether the defendants may seek relief from the trial court order pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3, and, in that regard, whether their challenge to the order demonstrates a substantial claim of a violation of their substantive rights, and whether the taking of the buccal swab by force, without any opportunity for appellate review of the order, would itself constitute an irremediable error.
