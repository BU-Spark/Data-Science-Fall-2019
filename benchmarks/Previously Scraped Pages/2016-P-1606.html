    11/30/2017   MEMORANDUM AND ORDER: The plaintiff, Reznik, has filed a notice of appeal in this court from a memorandum and order pursuant to Rule 1:28 allowing the defendants' request for appellate attorney's fees and double costs. See Reznik v. Urzia, 92 Mass. App. Ct. 1109 (2017). For the reasons that follow, we conclude the notice of appeal to be procedurally improper, and accordingly we strike the notice of appeal [2]. This action was brought by Reznik in the Concord District Court. Following a judgment in favor of the defendants and a dismissal of his appeal from the Appellate Division of the District Court, Reznik entered an appeal in this court. Both defendants filed an appellee brief in which they requested ". . . attorney[']s fees, pursuant to Fabre v. Walton, 441 Mass. 9, 10 (2004) and Yorke Mgt. v. Castro, 406 Mass. 17, 20 (1989), and damages and costs incurred in defending this appeal pursuant to Mass. [R.A.P.] 25 and 26." This court affirmed the Appellate Division's dismissal of the appeal and "allow[ed] the appellees' request for an award of appellate attorney's fees and double costs" concluding the appeal "appear[ed] to have been interposed solely for the purposes of harassment, wholly lacks legal or factual basis, and is otherwise frivolous." Reznik then filed a notice of appeal in this court seeking review by the Supreme Judicial Court of this court's award of appellate attorney's fees and costs. A notice of appeal is only proper when an appeal is "permitted by law." Mass. R.A.P. 3(a) as amended, 378 Mass. 927 (1979). An appeal from an award of costs made by a panel of the Appeals Court pursuant to Mass. R.A.P. 25 and G. L. c. 211A, § 15 is not permitted by law. Masterpiece Kitchen & Bath, Inc. v. Gordon, 425 Mass. 325, 330 n.11 (1997). Instead, "[a] party aggrieved by the award of costs by the Appeals Court under G. L. c. 211A, § 15, and Mass. R.A.P. 25 may seek review in [the Supreme Judicial Court] by applying for further appellate review." Ibid., citing Avery v. Steele, 414 Mass. 450, 451 (1993). Here, although not explicitly stated, the award of appellate attorney's fees was made, "as requested" by the defendants, pursuant to Mass. R.A.P. 25. The same is true for the award of double costs.[3] Contrary to the citations made in Reznik's notice of appeal, no award was requested or made pursuant to G. L. c. 231, § 6F to which an appeal would be permitted under G. L. c. 231, § 6G. Accordingly, Reznik may not seek review of either award through the filing of a notice of appeal in this court. Ibid. ("[A]n appeal from the denial of the plaintiff's motion under Mass. R.A.P. 25 and G. L. c. 211A, § 15, is not permitted under [G. L. c. 231,] § 6G."). Therefore, we strike the notice of appeal. See McMenimen v. Passatempo, 452 Mass. 178, 182 (2008). Reznik may seek further review of the award by filing an application for leave to obtain further appellate review. See Mass. R.A.P. 27.1. (Meade, J., Maldonado, J., Desmond, J.). *Notice Footnotes: 1. Miranda Equipment & Contracting, Inc. 2.We strike the notice of appeal independent of and for different reasons than those stated in the defendants' motion to strike, which argued the notice of appeal was premature. 3. This court may also award double costs in the case of a frivolous appeal pursuant to G. L. c. 211A, § 15. An award under that statute similarly may not be appealed. Gordon, 425 Mass. at 330 n.11. The court's award of "double costs" could not have been made pursuant to G. L. c. 231, § 6F because that statute does not provide for double costs. Compare G. L. c. 231, § 6G (court may award reasonable costs) with G. L. c. 211A, § 15 (court may award double costs).
