    12/12/2017 #12 MEMORANDUM AND ORDER In the early morning hours of August 21, 2015, the plaintiff went to the Salisbury police station and applied for an emergency abuse prevention order, pursuant to G. L. c. 209A, against her husband, the defendant. A judge issued the order, ex-parte, after a telephone hearing. The order required the defendant, among other things, to vacate the marital home and have no contact with the plaintiff or with their eight year old son until a full hearing, which was scheduled to take place at Newburyport District Court later that day. A different judge heard testimony from both parties that day and extended the order for a one-year period of time until August 22, 2016. The defendant appealed. On May 3, 2016, while the appeal was still pending, the plaintiff moved to terminate the order, explaining that the parties were divorcing and that the termination of the order would help them communicate in order to better co-parent. The court allowed the motion and ordered that "[l]aw enforcement agencies shall destroy all records of such [o]rder." On appeal, the defendant challenges the issuance of the ex parte order, as well as the later extension of the order on the basis that the evidence was insufficient to establish that the defendant had abused either the plaintiff or their son. We do not reach these issues as we determine that the appeal is moot.[1] See Quinn v. Gjoni, 89 Mass. App. Ct. 408, 414 (2016) (where G. L. c. 209A order has been vacated and law enforcement officials ordered to destroy records of such order, defendant obtained all relief to which he could be entitled). The defendant contends that the termination of the order does not moot the appeal, because the fact that the order issued in the first place might be used against him in a future child custody proceeding or a future proceeding alleging his abuse or neglect of his child. However, our case law recognizes that where the order being challenged has been vacated, and records of it directed to be destroyed, the case is moot because there is no further relief that can be awarded, even upon a successful appeal. See Allen v. Allen, 89 Mass. App. Ct. 403, 405-406 (2016). Contrast Seney v. Morhy, 467 Mass. 58, 62 (2014) (appeals from harassment prevention orders that merely have expired, like appeals from expired abuse prevention orders, should not be dismissed as moot). To the extent that we have discretionary authority to decide moot issues, we decline to do so in this case. Appeal dismissed as moot. (Milkey, Blake & Singh, JJ.[2]).
