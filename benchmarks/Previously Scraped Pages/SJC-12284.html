    03/23/2017 #7 ORDER: Jahmal Brangan is charged with armed robbery while masked in violation of G. L. c. 265, § 17. His trial ended in a mistrial, on his own motion, due to prosecutorial misconduct. In declaring a mistrial, the trial judge also ordered that the indictment would stand for retrial. See Commonwealth v. Brangan, 475 Mass. 143, 144 (2016). The Commonwealth appealed, arguing that the judge's ruling on the motion for mistrial issued, as it was, after the jury had reached a verdict amounted to an order on a motion for a new trial. See id. We concluded that "the timing of the order granting the defendant's motion for a mistrial, brought prior to the verdict, did not change the character of that motion," and that the Commonwealth was "not entitled to an appeal." Id. We also concluded that the issue whether a retrial would violate the defendant's double jeopardy rights was not properly before us because the defendant had not "appeal[ed] the trial judge's conclusion that double jeopardy would not bar a retrial, nor did he file a motion to dismiss his case based on the insufficiency of the evidence at the first trial." Id. at 149. We subsequently denied the defendant's petition for rehearing without prejudice to his filing a motion to dismiss in the trial court and, if that motion was denied, to seeking relief pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3. The defendant thereafter filed just such a motion in the trial court, which was denied. The defendant's subsequent petition to a single justice of this court pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3, was also denied. The case is now before us pursuant to S.J.C. Rule 2:21, as amended, 434 Mass. 1301 (2001), which requires a petitioner seeking relief from an interlocutory ruling of the trial court to "set forth the reasons why review of the trial court decision cannot adequately be obtained on appeal from any final adverse judgment in the trial court or by other available means." Although a defendant ordinarily is not entitled to interlocutory review of the denial of a motion to dismiss, we have recognized that "[a] criminal defendant who raises a double jeopardy claim of substantial merit is entitled to review of the claim before he is retried," and that G. L. c. 211, § 3, is the appropriate route for obtaining review. Neverson v. Commonwealth, 406 Mass. 174, 175 (1989). We have also recognized that "[t]he defendant . . . [has] the right to appeal an adverse determination by the single justice to the full court" in these circumstances. Id. at 176 n.2. See Creighton v. Commonwealth, 423 Mass. 1001 (1996) (recognizing appeal from single justice's decision to full court before retrial, as opposed to direct appeal from conviction following retrial, as preferred route for obtaining review of double jeopardy claim). Accordingly, the defendant's appeal from the single justice's judgment may proceed with full briefing and oral argument.
