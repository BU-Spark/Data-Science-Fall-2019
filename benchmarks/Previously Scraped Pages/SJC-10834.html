    11/07/2014   Oral argument held. (Gants, C.J., Cordy, J., Duffly, J., Lenk, J., Hines, J.).
    02/06/2015 #38 ORDER: This case is before the court on the defendant's direct appeal from his convictions of murder in the first degree, unlawful possession of a firearm, and unlawful possession of ammunition, and his appeals from the denial of his motions for postconviction discovery, for a new trial, and for an evidentiary hearing on his new trial motion. The defendant's primary argument, which he raised in the new trial motion, is that his trial counsel was ineffective because, among other things, counsel failed to offer available evidence that the defendant was intoxicated at the time of the shooting. He claims that such evidence was relevant and would have been helpful to his contention at trial that the shooting was accidental, or at most reckless, rather than intentional as the Commonwealth sought to prove. The Commonwealth responds, in part, by arguing that trial counsel's failure to offer evidence of intoxication was strategic and not manifestly unreasonable in the circumstances. The defendant's appellate counsel, Ms. O'Brien, who also filed the new trial motion in his behalf, represents that she tried repeatedly to contact the defendant's trial counsel, Mr. Murphy, in writing and by telephone, to discuss the claim of ineffectiveness, but that Mr. Murphy never responded to her inquiries. Ms. O'Brien avers that, after checking the Board of Bar Overseers website, she learned that trial counsel was then currently suspended from practice. Ms. O'Brien also avers that she intended to ask Mr. Murphy whether he saw and had copies of certain telephone records that are the subject of the defendant's request for postconviction discovery. Because of Mr. Murphy's unwillingness to cooperate, and because the trial judge denied the defendant's request for an evidentiary hearing on the motion for a new trial, there is no testimony from Mr. Murphy in the record, in the form of an affidavit or otherwise, on these matters. Having reviewed the record and considered the parties' arguments, this court is of the view that the resolution of the defendant's appeal will be facilitated by an evidentiary hearing on the defendant's new trial motion, at which the defendant can subpoena Mr. Murphy to testify. The trial judge, or, if he is retired, another judge of the trial court, will thus have an opportunity to hear from Mr. Murphy on the defendant's claim of ineffectiveness and on the unresolved question whether Mr. Murphy saw and had copies of the telephone records that are the subject of the postconviction discovery request, as well as receive any other relevant evidence on those issues, and this court will then have the benefit of factual findings on those matters. The case is therefore remanded to the trial court for an evidentiary hearing on the defendant's new trial motion. The appeal shall be stayed pending the conclusion of that hearing and a decision by the motion judge. The court expresses no view at this juncture on the substantive merits of the defendant's claims, other than to say that an evidentiary hearing on the new trial motion is in order. After the motion is decided, the parties will have an opportunity to submit supplemental memoranda in this court addressing the decision, and this court will then be in a position to resolve all of the claims before it. Until the motion is decided in the trial court, the defendant shall submit periodic status reports to the clerk of this court, in writing, at sixty-day intervals, regarding the progress of the matter in the trial court. The parties and the trial court should proceed expeditiously, with the understanding that this appeal, which already is more than four years old, is under advisement and is being stayed for this purpose. So ordered. By the Court,

    07/15/2015 #47 ORDER (in pertinent part): "[T]he Commonwealth shall immediately produce any records in its possession of the alleged victim's telephone calls (incoming and outgoing) on the night of the shooting. If the Commonwealth has no such records in its possession, it shall say so, and shall then work with the defendant's current counsel to obtain the relevant records from the alleged victim's cell phone provider, or, barring that, the defendant through counsel shall be permitted to conduct a forensic examination of the phone. A review of the records (or an examination of the phone in the absence of the records) should establish quickly and definitively whether a call was made to or received from the alleged victim's sister on the night of the shooting. After the defendant's counsel has had an opportunity to view the records or examine the phone, she may supplement the evidence at the April 16 hearing with this new evidence regarding the alleged victim's telephone calls. No other evidence need be taken. The judge will then be in a position to make factual findings based on the evidence heard at the April 16 hearing and the supplemental evidence, if any, presented by the defendant as a result of the examination of the telephone records (or the phone itself). In addition to finding facts, the judge shall rule on the motion anew in light of the facts found and shall determine whether the defendant is entitled to a new trial based on those facts. The judge's findings and rulings are to be transmitted to the clerk of this court as soon as they are available, at which point the stay will be lifted and the appeal will resume. In the meantime, the defendant shall continue to provide this court with periodic status reports regarding the progress of the proceedings in the Superior Court. We acknowledge the Commonwealth's argument that the cell phone was admitted as a trial exhibit and was, by all indications, fully available to the defense for examination prior to trial. However, that does not answer the questions at the heart of the defendant's ineffectiveness claim, namely, whether counsel in fact examined the phone and, if not, whether his failure to do so deprived the defendant of a substantial ground of defense. The defendant represents that, at the hearing on April 16, 2015, counsel testified that was not able to recall whether he had actually examined the phone's call history. Any further questions that may arise concerning the logistics of obtaining the phone records, or the logistics of examining the phone in the absence of the records, may be dealt with in the Superior Court. If an examination of the phone becomes necessary and an expert is required for that purpose, the judge may grant reasonable fees for an expert."
