    12/22/2011 #31 ORDER (By the Court): The defendant was convicted of murder in the first degree. In the appeal from his motion for a new trial, consolidated with his direct appeal, the defendant has raised a claim that denial of entry to the public generally and members of his family in particular during jury empanelment proceedings violated his right to a public trial in violation of the Sixth Amendment of the United States Constitution. The trial judge made no findings at trial concerning any closure of the courtroom. See Waller v. Georgia, 467 U.S. 39, 48 (1984). The motion judge, who also was the trial judge, determined that the defendant has presented "no credible evidence that any persons 'excluded' from the courtroom during jury selection ever brought that fact to the attention of the court, counsel, the defendant or anyone else responsible for the court at any time during or for three years after the trial." It is not clear from this statement whether the judge concluded that closure did or did not occur. The analysis undertaken by the judge is insufficient for meaningful consideration of the defendant's claim. Accordingly, this case is remanded for the purpose of obtaining findings regarding the defendant's Sixth Amendment claim. The judge may conduct an evidentiary hearing on the issue, but is not required to do so. The court requests that the judge address the following questions or topics in his findings, as well as any others that he deems relevant: (1) Which individuals, aside from prospective jurors, were present in the court room during the jury empanelment process? (2) Was anyone asked to leave the court room prior to the start of jury empanelment or denied entry after empanelment had begun? If so, by whom? (3) If persons were denied entry because prospective jurors were standing, were such persons allowed to enter after prospective jurors were dismissed during jury empanelment? (4) Was there a sign on the door of the court room stating that entry was prohibited? (5) Was the door by which the public entered the courtroom locked at any time during empanelment? If so, by whom, and at whose direction? (6) What actions did court officers take with respect to permitting or denying entry of individual persons to observe the empanelment process? (7) If a closure of the court room occurred, what interests was the closure intended to advance? (8) If closure of the courtroom occurred, for what period of time did the courtroom remain closed? (9) If a closure of the courtroom occurred, was the closure no broader than necessary to protect those interests? (10) If a closure of the court room occurred, were there alternatives to closure that would have protected those interests? (11) If a closure of the court room occurred, has the defendant shown good cause for delay between such closure and his objection? (12) If a closure of the court room occurred, was the defendant or defense counsel aware of the closure at the time? Should the defendant or defense counsel have been aware of the closure at the time? (13) If a closure of the court room occurred, was the defendant's non-objection or delayed-objection a matter of trial or appellate strategy? The court retains jurisdiction of this case. The judge is to report the findings requested in this order within sixty days of the date of the order. .
