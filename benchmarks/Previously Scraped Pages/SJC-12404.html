    11/03/2017 #3 ORDER: A grand jury has issued an eleven-count indictment against the defendant charging him with, among other things, armed assault with intent to murder, assault with a dangerous weapon, and several firearms offenses. The indictments also included armed career criminal charges pursuant to G. L. c. 269, § 10G (c), and habitual offender charges pursuant to G. L. c. 279, § 25 (a). The defendant moved to dismiss the armed career criminal and habitual offender aspects of the indictments. A judge in the Superior Court allowed the motion. The Commonwealth then sought relief from a single justice of this court pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3. The Commonwealth argued in its petition that the judge erred in dismissing the habitual offender portion of the indictments.[1] The single justice noted and appears to have accepted the Commonwealth's claim in its petition, based on its reading of Commonwealth v. Pelletier, 449 Mass. 392, 395-396 (2007), that, because the motion judge did not dismiss the indictments in their entirety, it "appear[ed] that there may be no right of appeal" from the judge's ruling pursuant to Mass. R. Crim. P. 15 (a) (1), and that a petition for general superintendence relief pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3, was the Commonwealth's only recourse. The single justice therefore proceeded to decide the petition on its substantive merits, denying it. The Commonwealth has appealed to the full court from the judgment of the single justice. The case is now before this court pursuant to S.J.C. Rule 2:21, as amended, 434 Mass. 1301 (2001). Because the underlying trial court order is interlocutory, the rule requires the Commonwealth, as the petitioner, to "set forth the reasons why review of the trial court decision cannot adequately be obtained on appeal from any final adverse judgment in the trial court or by other available means." Rule 2:21 (2). In its memorandum, the Commonwealth states, as it did before the single justice, that it appears to have no right to appeal pursuant to Mass. R. Crim. P. 15 (a) (1) from a judge's pretrial dismissal of habitual offender charges within an indictment, and therefore that it could not have obtained review of the judge's order in this case by any means other than its petition under G. L. c. 211, § 3. It cites Commonwealth v. Pelletier, supra, to support that position. Recognizing that review under c. 211, § 3, is not automatic even where there is no other remedy, the Commonwealth further claims that review is warranted in this case because the case "presents an important question of statutory interpretation" that "impacts the Commonwealth's ability to indict under G. L. c. 279, § 25 (a)."[2] We are satisfied that the Commonwealth's appeal should be allowed to proceed in the ordinary course, with briefing and oral argument, particularly where the single justice decided the Commonwealth's petition on the substantive merits. In addition to addressing the merits of the habitual offender issue, however, the parties should also be sure to address in their briefs, as threshold procedural issues, (1) whether Commonwealth v. Pelletier, supra, stands for the proposition stated by the Commonwealth, that the Commonwealth has no right to appeal from a pretrial dismissal of habitual offender charges in circumstances like this, and, if so, whether Pelletier was correctly decided; and (2) whether the court should hold that, going forward, the Commonwealth has a right to appeal pursuant to Mass. R. A. P. 15 (a) (1) in these circumstances. By the Court [1] The Commonwealth does not contest the dismissal of the armed career criminal portion of the indictments. [2] The underlying issue concerns the timing and sequence of the convictions needed to support an habitual offender charge under G. L. c. 279, § 25 (a), and the application of our recent decisions in Commonwealth v. Resende, 474 Mass. 455 (2016), and Commonwealth v. Garvey, 477 Mass. 59 (2017), to the issue.
    11/03/2017 #4 ORDER: The Commonwealth's brief is due on or before December 15, 2017. The defendant's brief is due within 30 days after service of the Commonwealth's brief. By the Court.
