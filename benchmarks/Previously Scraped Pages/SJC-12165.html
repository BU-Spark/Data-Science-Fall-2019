    12/27/2016 #6 ORDER: The defendant, Stanley Sharris, was indicted for first degree murder in 1995. He was found to be, and remains to this day, not competent to stand trial. The parties agree that this will not change -- that is, he will never be competent to stand trial. He is now seventy-two years old. He has filed several motions to dismiss the indictment in the Superior Court, each of which has been denied. After the most recent motion was denied, in March, 2016, Sharris filed a petition for relief pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3, in the county court. The single justice denied the petition without a hearing, and the case is now before this court pursuant to S.J.C. Rule 2:21, as amended, 434 Mass. 1301 (2001). As a general rule, a defendant in a criminal case cannot appeal from the denial of a motion to dismiss until after trial, at which point, if there is a conviction, the defendant may seek review of the conviction as well as the denial of the motion to dismiss. General Laws G. L. c. 211, § 3, "may not be used to circumvent this rule." Jackson v. Commonwealth, 437 Mass. 1008, 1009 (2002) ("Unless a single justice decides the matter on the merits or reserves and reports it to the full court . . . a defendant cannot receive review under G. L. c. 211, § 3, from the denial of his motion to dismiss"). See Ventresco v. Commonwealth, 409 Mass. 82, 83-84 (1991). Sharris recognizes this, but argues that because he will never be tried -- because he will never be competent to stand trial -- he will never have an opportunity to seek review of any conviction or of the denial of his motions to dismiss. Furthermore, G. L. c. 123, § 16 (f), which provides for the dismissal in certain circumstances of criminal charges against individuals found incompetent to stand trial, does not appear to apply to Sharris. Pursuant to that statute, the date on which a criminal charge against an incompetent defendant may be dismissed turns on the date on which the defendant, if tried for and convicted of the greatest charge, would be eligible for parole. Because Sharris has been charged with first degree murder, there is no parole eligibility date. In Sharris's view, therefore, he has no adequate alternative remedy and he is thus entitled to review pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3. Given the circumstances, we are satisfied at this preliminary juncture that Sharris's appeal from the single justice's denial of his G. L. c. 211, § 3, petition should be allowed to proceed in the full court. In its opposition to Sharris's G. L. c. 211, § 3, petition, the Commonwealth argued that Sharris does have an adequate alternative remedy: because he has been found incompetent to stand trial, he is entitled to annual hearings to challenge his commitment pursuant to G. L. c. 123, § 16 (c); and he may also appeal from any recommitment decision. Any such appeal, however, pertains only to Sharris's incompetency and the ongoing need for commitment on that basis, and does not pertain to his claimed right to dismissal of the underlying criminal charge against him. It is questionable, therefore, whether such an appeal from a criminal competency determination would constitute an adequate alternative remedy for the specific claim made here. The Commonwealth is, however, free to press its argument in its brief on appeal if it wishes to do so, in addition to addressing the substantive merits of Sharris's claim that the indictment should be dismissed, and we will decide the point after it has been fully briefed. In accordance with the Rules of Appellate Procedure, Sharris shall serve and file his brief within forty days of the date of this order, and the Commonwealth, in turn, shall serve and file its brief within thirty days thereafter. By the Court.
